## Glossary of Terms

<details close><summary>Glossary</summary>

**automation** — The use of tools to complete activities without, or with minimal, human intervention. *(Cambridge English Dictionary [1])*

**critical realism** — Suggests that social facts are constructed by people in their context and time, and that the approval and acceptance of facts is particular to context and time. *[2]*

**data-driven research** — Uses both primary and secondary sources. Primary data is directly drawn from the participants or materials produced by them. Secondary data is drawn from published work about the participants, such as academic literature. *[3]*

**decision table test** — Decision Table Testing is used to design tests based on a model of the behaviour to be tested as a set of logical rules, typically expressed as Boolean logic in a table of causes and effects. *[4]*

**DISC** — DISC model or DISC profile for communication styles: D is for Dominance, I is for Influence, S is for Steadiness, and C is for Conscientiousness. People use a mix of the styles and may move between them, while having a preference, usually between two or more. See https://www.discprofile.com/disc-styles

**happy path** — Testing a scenario in which "everything happens as it is supposed to with no faults." *(Cambridge English Dictionary [1])*

**heuristic** — A rule of thumb that aids problem-solving, providing answers that are often but not always correct, and thus are fast but fallible in use. *[5]*

**human-centred design** — "An approach to interactive systems development that aims to make systems usable and useful by focusing on the users, their needs and requirements, and by applying human factors/ergonomics, and usability knowledge and techniques. This approach enhances effectiveness and efficiency, improves human well-being, user satisfaction, accessibility and sustainability; and counteracts possible adverse effects of use on human health, safety and performance." *(ISO 9241 [6])*

**illusion of usability** — The belief that (1) usability is superficial (i.e. an attractive interface, single personas suffice, people do not grow and change), and (2) that usability is the same as quality in use and sufficient for a good user experience. See Chapter 5 of the dissertation.

**interpretivism** — Allows the participants in the research to express themselves through stories and narratives. Perceptions, interpretations, and insights expressed by participants or the researcher, based on the stories, constitute knowledge. *[7]*

**lived experience (LX)** — "Repercussions extending into the users' daily lives and away from the technology itself." *(Porter [8])*

**mixed methods** — The incorporation of qualitative and quantitative approaches in a single study, drawing on the strengths of each. *[3]*

**non-hypothesis question** — An open question that does not expect or prompt for a "correct answer." *(Cynefin wiki)*

**persona** — Fictional characters, created based on research, which represent the different user types that might use a service, product, site, or brand in a similar way. *[9]*

**pragmatism** — Practical know-how contributes to knowledge, and that knowledge can be used to inform practice, in a given context, to solve problems and take action to improve practice. *[10]*

**quality in use** — Composed of five characteristics that relate to the outcome of interaction when a product is used in a particular context of use. This system model is applicable to the complete human-computer system, including both computer systems in use and software products in use. *(ISO 25010 [11])*

**quality viewpoints** — The quality viewpoints are Manufacturing-based, Product-based, User-based, Value-based, and Transcendent-based. *[12–14]*

**researcher-driven research** — Uses models conceptualised by the researcher, for example mind maps and concepts. *[3]*

**shelfware** — Software tools obtained but not used. For example, "developers often struggle to interact with the interfaces of these tools, leading to tool abandonment, and consequently the proliferation of preventable vulnerabilities." *[15]*

**state transition test** — Uses a graphic model of the system that shows events that cause a transition between states, and triggers for those events, which is translated into a state table to define tests for each state and transition. *[16]*

**testing** — Any activities that aid understanding of risk and quality for software, systems, infrastructure, business processes, organisations, and people's experiences of those artefacts, risks and qualities. Testing is defined in multiple ways in industry, depending on viewpoint.

**testing viewpoints** — The testing viewpoints are: Analytical, Standard, Quality, Agile, and Context-driven. *[17–19]*

**tool** — A piece of equipment used by a person to enable them to carry out a task. *(Cambridge English Dictionary [1])*

**tools to support testing (TsST)** — Any tool to support testing activities, including automation.

**Unix Philosophy** — A set of design principles emphasising simplicity, modularity, and clarity in software development. Core tenets include: Do One Thing Well; Compose Tools; Use Clear and Simple Interfaces; Prioritise Simplicity; Empower the User. *[20]*

**usability** — A measure of how well a specific user in a specific context can use a product/design to achieve a defined goal effectively, efficiently and satisfactorily. Usability contributes to user experience. *(Interaction Design Foundation)*

**user experience** — "A person's perceptions and responses that result from the use or anticipated use of a product, system or service." *(ISO 9241 [6])* User experience is concerned with the entire process of acquiring and integrating a product, including aspects of branding, design, usability, and function.

**wicked problem** — "A wicked problem has innumerable causes, is tough to describe, and doesn't have a right answer." *[21]*

**Zachman Framework** — A framework for the strategy for enterprise architecture. See https://zachman-feac.com/zachman/about-the-zachman-framework
